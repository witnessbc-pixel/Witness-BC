Editorial principles

Principles

A small newsroom only works if the rules are explicit. These principles protect readers, sources, and credibility.

1) Truth and verification

Claims are proportional to evidence. When certainty is not possible, uncertainty is stated plainly.

  • Source hierarchy: documents and on-the-record sources first.
  • Cross-checking: at least two independent confirmations when feasible.
  • Context: timelines, dates, and definitions are explicit.

2) Fairness and right of reply

Criticism is paired with a meaningful opportunity to respond, within reasonable time.

  • Accurate framing: no cherry-picking or misleading excerpts.
  • Respect: no hate speech, no humiliation, no sensationalism.
  • Proportionality: tone and prominence match the facts.

3) Independence

Funding does not buy coverage, outcomes, or favourable treatment.

  • No pay-for-coverage: support never guarantees a story.
  • No editorial control: funders do not approve drafts.
  • Conflict discipline: conflicts are disclosed or the work is declined.

4) Privacy and safety

Identity protection is a default, not a special request—especially for vulnerable sources.

  • Minimize identifiers: publish the least identifying detail needed.
  • Consent: no personal story is used as content without explicit consent.
  • Risk assessment: publication choices consider retaliation and harm.

5) Corrections and transparency

Errors happen. The test is how they are handled. Corrections are visible, dated, and specific.

  • Corrections log: maintain a page that records changes and dates.
  • Clear labels: “Correction”, “Clarification”, or “Update”.
  • Reader contact: a direct channel to flag potential errors.
